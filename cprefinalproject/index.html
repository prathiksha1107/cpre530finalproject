TCP/IP Model

TCP/IP stands for Transmission Control Protocol/ Internet Protocol which is a set of networking protocols that allows two or more computers to communicate. There are four layers of the TCP/IP model: network access, internet, transport, and application. Used together, these layers are a suite of protocols.

Physical Layer

The TCP/IP model, essential for internet communication, operates through four layers when transmitting data like messages, photos, or files. Initially, data is divided into packets which pass through the layers sequentially: starting with the Application layer, then the Transport (TCP) layer, followed by the Network (IP) layer, and finally the Physical/Data Link layer. On reaching the destination, these data packets traverse back in reverse order through the layers as they are reassembled. This process ensures the data is correctly segmented, transmitted, and reconstructed, maintaining both the integrity and efficiency of the communication over the internet.

Question And Answers:

What is a protocol?

A protocol is a set of rules that govern how systems communicate. For networking, they govern how data is transferred from one system to another.

What is a protocol Suite?

A protocol suite is a collection of protocols that are designed to work together.

Analogy between the postal network and TCP/IP

The postal system is a complex network designed for the efficient and secure delivery of mail and parcels. At its core, it involves the collection, sorting, transportation, and delivery of letters and packages. This system starts with individuals or businesses sending various types of mail, which are then collected by local post offices. These post offices sort the mail based on destinations, forwarding them through a network of transportation links—trucks, planes, and trains—to reach regional or central sorting facilities. Here, the mail is further sorted and dispatched to local post offices near the recipients. Finally, mail carriers deliver the mail to the specific addresses, ensuring each piece reaches its intended recipient.

Drawing a parallel to the TCP/IP model, the processes of the postal system reflect the functions of different layers in digital communication. Composing and sending a letter or package resembles the Application Layer, where data is prepared and managed for transmission. The sorting and transportation of mail through various local post offices and the local mailmen correspond to the Network Layer, managing the routing and delivery of data packets to specific IP addresses. The Transport Layer, comparable to a postal tracking system, ensures efficient and error-free delivery of these packets. Lastly, the physical infrastructure of the postal system, including trucks, planes, and mail carriers, mirrors the Physical/Datalink Layer in the TCP/IP model, responsible for the actual transmission of data and direct node-to-node transfer protocols.

The TCP/IP model for digital communication is similar to how a postal system works. Imagine:

  1. Application Layer: Like writing and sending a letter, this layer is where information is created and prepared for sending over the internet.
  2. Network Layer: Similar to how post offices sort and send mail to the right places, this layer directs internet data to the correct addresses.
  3. Transport Layer: Just as a mail tracking system ensures mail is delivered correctly, this layer makes sure data reaches its destination without problems.
  4. Physical/Datalink Layer: Comparable to the trucks and mail carriers that physically deliver mail, this layer handles the actual movement of data from one computer to another.

Data Breaches in the U.S. Cost Twice as Much as the Global Average

Our cyber stats begin with a reminder of the financial cost of data breaches. According to IBM’s 2022 Cost of a Data Breach report, the average cost of a data breach in the United States is $9.44 million, more than twice the global average of $4.35 million and highest overall. According to the report, the United States has held the title for highest cost per breach for the past 12 years.

Human Error Accounts for 95% of Cyber Attacks

One common thread running through the cyber security threat landscape is the undeniable fact that humans make mistakes. Research indicates 19 out of 20 breaches are the result of human error in some form. This includes a range of human behaviors like downloading infected software, weak or irresponsible password management or failing to install software updates that contain critical security patches.

In the United States, data breaches cost more than anywhere else in the world, with the average attack costing $4.24 million

According to IBM’s latest Cost of Data Breach Report, the average total cost of a data breach increased by nearly 10% since last year. That’s the biggest single-year jump in the last seven years. Organizations with a more mature security posture fared better than those who lag behind. What is often immeasurable is the damage caused from a tarnished brand reputation and diminished customer loyalty.
